Training Foundations

The 7 Principles of Training

Every training method is built on these universal principles. Understanding them is what separates a coach from someone who just writes workouts.

1
Progressive Overload — The body adapts to stress. To continue improving, the stimulus must progressively increase — through load, volume, density, range of motion, or complexity. Without overload, training produces maintenance at best.
2
Specificity (SAID Principle) — Specific Adaptations to Imposed Demands. The body adapts specifically to the stress placed upon it. A marathon runner will not develop maximal strength from running. Train what you want to improve.
3
Reversibility (Use It or Lose It) — Adaptations are lost when training stops. Cardiovascular fitness declines within 2–3 weeks; strength within 3–5 weeks. Motor patterns are retained longer than physiological adaptations.
4
Individuality — Every person responds differently based on genetics, training age, hormonal environment, recovery capacity, sleep, nutrition, and stress. Cookie-cutter programmes work for some and fail others.
5
Supercompensation / Recovery — Adaptation occurs during rest, not during training. Training is the stimulus; nutrition and sleep are the raw materials; rest is when the adaptation is built. Insufficient recovery leads to overtraining and injury.
6
Variation (Accommodation) — The body adapts and becomes efficient at repeated stimuli. Varying exercise selection, rep ranges, tempo, or modality prevents accommodation plateaus and maintains progress.
7
Whole-Body Development — Muscular imbalances, movement pattern deficiencies, and neglected energy systems create weaknesses and injury risk. Balanced programming addresses antagonist muscles, mobility, stability, and multiple energy systems.

Energy Systems

ATP-PCr (Phosphagen): 0–10 seconds. Explosive, maximal effort. Sprint starts, single heavy lifts. Replenishes in 3–5 minutes full rest.

Glycolytic (Anaerobic): 10 sec – 2 min. High-intensity effort. 400m sprint, sets of 8–15 reps. Produces lactate as by-product.

Oxidative (Aerobic): 2+ minutes. Sustained effort. Distance running, long sport rallies. Primary recovery system between efforts.

Hypertrophy Drivers

Mechanical Tension: The primary driver. Force generated by muscle fibres under load, especially in lengthened (stretched) positions. Heavy compound and isolation work under load.

Metabolic Stress: The "pump" — accumulation of metabolites creating cell swelling and anabolic signalling. Moderate load, higher reps, shorter rest.

Muscle Damage: Eccentric-phase microtrauma triggers satellite cell activation and repair. The least important of the three — soreness does not equal growth.

Strength & Conditioning

What is Strength & Conditioning?

S&C is the application of exercise science to improve athletic performance. Unlike bodybuilding (aesthetics) or powerlifting (max strength in 3 lifts), S&C develops the complete athletic profile — strength, power, speed, agility, conditioning, mobility, and injury resilience. It underpins performance in every sport.

Maximal strength is the absolute amount of force a neuromuscular system can produce. It is the foundation of all other strength qualities — you cannot express power without strength. Every S&C programme must include a maximal strength phase.

Neuromuscular adaptations: Increased motor unit recruitment, improved rate coding (motor units firing faster), enhanced inter-muscular coordination, and reduced neural inhibition (Golgi tendon organ becomes less restrictive over time).

Key lifts: Back squat, front squat, trap bar deadlift, Romanian deadlift, bench press, overhead press, weighted pull-up, hip thrust, single-leg variations. True max strength work requires 48–72 hours CNS recovery between sessions targeting the same movement pattern.

Power = Force × Velocity. A strong athlete is not automatically powerful. Power training develops the ability to express force rapidly — critical for jumping, sprinting, throwing, and explosive changes of direction.

Plyometrics: Jump training utilising the stretch-shortening cycle (SSC) — elastic energy stored when muscles are rapidly pre-stretched before concentric contraction. Box jumps, depth jumps, hurdle hops, broad jumps, med ball throws. Depth jumps are the most potent plyometric stimulus — step off a box, absorb rapidly, immediately jump maximally. Ground contact time is the key variable.

Olympic Lifting (Clean, Snatch, Jerk): The most powerful movements for developing athletic power. The clean & jerk and snatch require maximal force production in the pulling phase followed by rapid repositioning under the bar. Triple extension (ankle, knee, hip) directly transfers to sprint mechanics and jumping. Require significant technical coaching.

Contrast training: Pairing a heavy strength lift (e.g. back squat at 85%) immediately with a plyometric movement (e.g. box jump). The post-activation potentiation (PAP) effect from the heavy lift temporarily enhances power output in the explosive movement.

Speed development: Sprint mechanics, acceleration (0–10m), max velocity (30–60m), and speed endurance. Early acceleration is about force application and forward lean; max velocity requires hip flexor strength, ground contact mechanics, and stride length. Resisted sprints (sled), assisted sprints (overspeed), and sprint drills (A-skip, B-skip, high knees) develop different speed aspects.

Agility: True agility involves a reactive element — responding to external stimuli (opponent, ball). Planned agility (cone drills, ladders) builds movement patterns but differs from reactive agility. T-test, 5-10-5 shuttle, and reactive cone drills are practical tools.

Coaching note: SAQ must follow maximal strength and power work in session order — fatigue destroys movement quality. Never programme SAQ after heavy metabolic conditioning.

HIIT: Alternating maximal effort with rest or active recovery. Improves VO₂ max, anaerobic threshold, and cardiac output simultaneously. Classic ratios: 1:1 (30s on:30s off), 1:2 (20s on:40s off for power athletes), 1:4 (10s on:40s off for true speed work). Wingate protocol: 30s all-out sprint × 4–6.

LISS: Extended aerobic work at 60–70% max HR. Builds aerobic base — improves cardiac stroke volume, mitochondrial density, fat oxidation. The aerobic system is the recovery system for all other energy systems. Even power athletes benefit from aerobic base work in off-season.

Alactic power training (ATP-PCr): Very short, maximally explosive efforts with full recovery. E.g. 6-second sprints with 54-second rest × 6–10 sets. Develops the phosphagen system without accumulating fatigue that compromises quality. Used with sprinters, rugby backs, court sport athletes.

Mobility, Stability & Corrective Exercise
The foundation of sustainable performance and injury prevention
Prehab
▼

Mobility vs. Flexibility: Flexibility is passive range of motion. Mobility is active range of motion — the ability to control movement through a range. Athletes need mobility, not just flexibility. Hip mobility, thoracic rotation, ankle dorsiflexion, and shoulder internal rotation are the most commonly limiting factors.

Stability: The neuromuscular system's ability to maintain joint position under load. Core stability (transversus abdominis, multifidus), knee stability (VMO, glute medius), and scapular stability are critical for safe loading of the spine and extremities.

FMS (Functional Movement Screen): A 7-test movement assessment tool identifying movement asymmetries and limitations. Scores guide corrective exercise priorities. Athletes scoring <14/21 or any asymmetry on a single pattern are at higher injury risk.

Corrective programming: 10–15 min warm-up addressing client-specific deficits. Hip circles, 90/90 hip stretch, thoracic rotation, banded clamshells, dead bugs, bird dogs, and ankle mobility work are commonly included.

Powerlifting

What is Powerlifting?

Powerlifting is a strength sport contested across three lifts: back squat, bench press, and deadlift. Athletes compete for the highest combined total. Despite the name, powerlifting is about maximal strength (force), not power (force × velocity). Training revolves around progressive overload of these three movements with accessory work to address weaknesses and prevent injury.

The Big Three — Technique Overview
Squat, Bench Press, Deadlift — what makes them effective
Core
▼

Back Squat: High-bar (Olympic — more upright torso, deeper squat, quad dominant) vs. low-bar (powerlifting — more forward lean, hip/posterior chain dominant). Competition squat requires hip crease below the top of the knee. Setup: big breath into diaphragm (Valsalva manoeuvre), tight brace, controlled descent, drive through floor on ascent.

Bench Press: Pectorals, anterior deltoid, triceps. Powerlifting bench allows leg drive and substantial arch. Setup: retract and depress scapulae before unracking, maintain full-foot contact with floor, drive heels into floor on the press. Grip width, bar path, and elbow angle vary by individual anthropometry.

Deadlift: Conventional (narrower stance, arms outside legs — hip extension dominant) vs. sumo (wide stance, arms inside legs — more quad and adductor involvement, shorter ROM). Both require neutral spine, lat engagement ("put shoulder blades in your back pockets"), and leg drive — it's a push off the floor, not a pull with the back.

Boris Sheiko is a legendary Russian powerlifting coach. His methodology is built on extremely high training volume at submaximal intensities — never training to failure, typically staying at 70–85% 1RM across many sets. The Big Three are trained 3–4 times per week each.

Core principle: Technique is everything. The high volume at submaximal loads allows massive repetition of competitive movements, ingraining motor patterns deeply while managing CNS fatigue. Sheiko believed training to failure degrades technique and is counterproductive for skill development. 80–120 lifts per session is not unusual. Classic programmes: #29, #30, #32.

Developed by Louie Simmons at Westside Barbell. Borrowed from Soviet sports science (Verkhoshansky). The conjugate method trains multiple physical qualities simultaneously rather than in sequential phases.

Max Effort (ME) days: Work up to a true 1–3RM on a variation of the squat/deadlift (lower day) and bench (upper day). The variation changes every 1–2 weeks — box squats, safety bar squats, rack pulls, close-grip bench, board press etc. Prevents accommodation while training maximal force.

Dynamic Effort (DE) days: 8–12 sets of 2 reps at 50–70% 1RM as explosively as possible, against band or chain resistance (accommodating resistance). Develops rate of force development (RFD). Accessory work targets specific weak points in each lift (weak off floor in deadlift → deficit deadlifts). GPP — sled dragging, prowler, kettlebell swings — for conditioning and recovery.

5/3/1 uses a 4-week wave: Week 1: 3×5 (65%, 75%, 85%+). Week 2: 3×3 (70%, 80%, 90%+). Week 3: 5/3/1 (75%, 85%, 95%+). Week 4: Deload. The "+" sets are AMRAP — the primary progress metric. After each cycle, training max increases 5 lbs (upper) or 10 lbs (lower).

Training max: Based on 90% of actual 1RM — conservative to ensure consistent long-term progression. Assistance templates: Boring But Big (BBB: 5×10 at 50–60%), First Set Last (FSL: repeat first working set 3–5 times), Jokers (post-AMRAP, continue adding weight in singles while still strong).

RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) and RIR (Reps In Reserve) allow training intensity to adjust based on daily performance capacity. Rather than prescribing a fixed percentage, the coach prescribes "4 sets of 4 at RPE 8" — the athlete finds the weight leaving approximately 2 reps in reserve.

Why it works: Sleep quality, stress, and nutrition all affect daily training capacity. A percentage-based programme may prescribe 85% 1RM on a fatigued day — this could be RPE 9–10 when it should be RPE 8. Auto-regulation allows appropriate relative intensity regardless of daily fluctuations.

Renaissance Periodisation (RP): Mike Israetel's framework using MEV (Minimum Effective Volume), MAV (Maximum Adaptive Volume), and MRV (Maximum Recoverable Volume) alongside RPE to manage fatigue and maximise adaptation. One of the most scientifically rigorous programming frameworks available.

Sports-Specific Training

Football (Soccer) — Physical Profile

Elite outfield players cover 10–14 km per match, 2–3 km high-intensity, 200–400m maximal sprinting. Demands: aerobic endurance (VO₂ max 55–70 ml/kg/min), repeated sprint ability, agility, and power. Position-specific variation is significant — central midfielders cover the most distance; centre-backs prioritise power and aerial dominance.

Football S&C Programme Structure
In-season, pre-season, and off-season periodisation for footballers
▼

Pre-season (6–8 weeks): Build aerobic base (LISS, shuttle runs), introduce strength training (compound lifts), progress to power work (plyometrics), increase load progressively. Speed work with ball-specific drills in final 2 weeks.

In-season: Maintenance of pre-season qualities. Max 2 gym sessions/week. MD-3 (3 days before match): power/strength maintenance — low volume, high intensity. MD-2: CNS activation — very low volume, fast movements. MD-1: Technical/tactical only, no gym. Aerobic conditioning maintained through match play.

Key exercises: Nordic hamstring curl (primary prevention for hamstring strains — the #1 football injury), single-leg RDL, trap bar deadlift, hip thrust, box jump, sled sprint, Copenhagen adductor exercises, rotational med ball throws.

Fitness tests: Yo-Yo Intermittent Recovery Test (IRT2) — primary aerobic benchmark. 30–15 IFT — widely used in elite academies. 10m and 30m sprint times. Countermovement jump (CMJ) — monitored weekly as a fatigue/readiness indicator.

Rugby Union/League — Physical Profile

One of the most physically demanding team sports. Forwards: powerful, strong, collision-dominant. Backs: fast, agile, powerful. Union forwards cover 5–7 km, backs 7–9 km per match. Over 50 collisions per forward per game. Contact forces of 500–1000N in tackles and scrums.

Rugby Training Programme Design
Position-specific programming for forwards and backs
▼

Forwards — Physical Priorities: Maximal strength (scrummaging, mauling), hypertrophy (mass = force in contact), repeated power (ruck clearouts), aerobic capacity for 80 minutes. Back squat, front squat, heavy RDL, trap bar deadlift, heavy bench press, pull-up progressions, loaded carries (farmer's, zercher — replicates ball carry resistance), scrum machine work.

Backs — Physical Priorities: Speed and acceleration (0–30m), reactive agility, power (step and beat defenders), repeated sprint ability. Olympic lifting variations (power clean, hang clean), plyometrics (depth jumps, bounding), sprint work, COD mechanics. Less emphasis on maximal strength, more on power-to-weight ratio.

In-season structure: Tuesday (MD+2) — heavy strength, GPS data-driven volume. Thursday (MD-3) — power work. Friday (MD-2) — speed and activation only. Match day Saturday/Sunday. All in-season gym work biased toward fast, powerful movement.

Injury prevention priorities: Neck strengthening (scrummaging, tackle), shoulder stability (rotator cuff), hamstring and groin (adductor squeezes, Copenhagen, Nordic curls), ankle proprioception. High collision frequency means chronic microtrauma — soft tissue management is essential.

Golf — Physical Profile

Golf is a rotational power sport requiring fine motor control. Elite players generate club head speeds of 100–120 mph. Physical demands: rotational power, hip mobility, thoracic rotation, shoulder mobility, core stability (anti-rotation), grip strength, and postural endurance for 18 holes.

Golf Performance Training
Building the physical qualities that translate directly to club head speed and swing consistency
▼

Rotational Power: The #1 performance quality in golf. The power transfer chain: ground push → hips → thoracic spine → arms. Rotational med ball throws (standing, kneeling, seated), rotational cable chops and lifts, landmine rotations. Weak links anywhere cost club head speed.

Hip Mobility: Restricted hip internal rotation (trail hip in backswing) is the most common physical limitation in amateur golfers. 90/90 hip stretch, pigeon pose, hip CARs (Controlled Articular Rotations). Improve hip mobility before adding rotational power — power through restricted ROM builds compensation patterns.

Thoracic Rotation: The thoracic spine must rotate for a full shoulder turn. Poor thoracic mobility forces the lower back to compensate — primary cause of golf-related lower back pain. Thoracic extension on foam roller, open book rotations, thread the needle.

Anti-rotation core stability: The core resists unwanted rotation during the swing. Pallof press (all variations), plank variations, dead bug, single-arm carries. Not sit-ups — the golf swing does not use spinal flexion.

Key exercises: Romanian deadlift, single-leg RDL (balance and hip stability), lat pulldown/pull-up, cable hip rotation, Turkish get-up, trap bar carry. TPI (Titleist Performance Institute) assessment is the industry standard for golf-specific physical screening.

Row 4×250m — 90s rest. Rowing 1000m is one of the most gruelling stations. Train it specifically with short powerful intervals.

Burpee broad jump 3×15m — practice efficient jump distance. Each short jump adds metres to the 80m race target.

Warm-up: 1km easy run. Upper body mobility, shoulder circles, band pull-aparts.

Sled Pull 5×20m at race weight — 2 min rest. Face away from sled, low walk. Keep tension constant on the straps.

Wall Ball 5×25 reps — 90s rest. Practice unbroken sets. Find a rhythm: squat down as the ball returns, don't wait for it. Aim to do race volume (100 reps) across sets.

Ski Erg 5×200m — 90s rest. Damper 4–5. Strong pull-through with hips — don't just use arms. Consistent stroke timing.

Farmer's Carry 6×50m at race weight — 90s rest. Walk fast — don't jog. Grip until your hands scream, then keep going.

Grip endurance finisher: Dead hang 3×max duration. Grip failing on the farmers carry costs 30+ seconds in a race.

Light movement (20 min): 10 min easy walk. 3×100m easy strides at 60%. Light stretching only — no intense mobility work the day before a race or hard session.

Station review: Mentally walk through each station. Where do you lose time? Where do you gain it? Pacing plan for the sled push (the most commonly underestimated station — it destroys more competitors than any other).

Equipment check: Confirm weight registration is correct for your category. Check wrist strap. Kit packed: running shoes (lightweight), workout shoes (for stations if separate), gloves if used for farmers carry, chalk.

Nutrition pre-race: High carbohydrate — 5–7g/kg body weight today. 3L+ water. Pre-race meal 3–4 hours before start: oats or pasta + protein. Caffeine 45 min before race (3–6mg/kg) — one of the most evidence-backed performance supplements for endurance events.

Sleep: 8+ hours. HYROX finish times correlate strongly with sleep quality — the running legs require maintained cognitive function and motor control in a fatigued state.

Bodybuilding

What is Bodybuilding Training?

Bodybuilding training systematically develops muscle hypertrophy, symmetry, and aesthetics. The goal is physical appearance, not performance. This shapes every decision: exercise selection is based on muscle development rather than functional carryover; rep ranges, rest periods, and intensity techniques are chosen to maximise hypertrophic stimulus; volume is substantially higher than other modalities.

Hypertrophy Science — Volume, Frequency & Intensity
What the research tells us about maximising muscle growth
Science
▼

Volume is the primary driver: Hard sets per muscle group per week is the most important variable for hypertrophy. Evidence suggests 10–20 sets per muscle group per week is the effective range for most trainees, with advanced athletes potentially benefiting from 20+ sets for lagging body parts. Below 10 sets/week is generally insufficient for maximum growth.

Frequency: Protein synthesis in a trained muscle peaks 24–36 hours after training and returns to baseline within 48–72 hours. Hitting each muscle group twice per week allows more total sets without excessive per-session fatigue. Research supports 2x/week per muscle group as the sweet spot for most trainees.

Proximity to failure: The final 5 reps before concentric failure are the most hypertrophic — they recruit the maximum number of high-threshold motor units. Sets stopping more than 4–5 reps short of failure are largely wasted for hypertrophy. You don't need to train to failure on every set, but RPE 8–9 minimum on most work sets is necessary.

Range of motion: Recent evidence strongly supports training through full ROM, with particular emphasis on the lengthened position. Exercises that load the muscle in the stretched position (incline dumbbell curl for biceps, Romanian deadlift for hamstrings, deep squat for quads) produce greater hypertrophy per set than those loading at the shortened position.

Intensity Techniques
Advanced methods to increase stimulus, break plateaus, and maximise volume efficiency
Intermediate+
▼

Drop Sets: Perform a set to near-failure, immediately reduce weight by 20–30%, continue to near-failure without rest, repeat 1–2 more times. Dramatically increases volume per unit of time. Best used on isolation exercises at the end of a session — avoid on heavy compound lifts where technique degrades under fatigue.

Supersets: Two exercises back-to-back with minimal rest. Antagonist supersets (bicep curl → tricep extension) allow one muscle to recover while its antagonist works — more work in less time without compromising either exercise. Agonist supersets (two exercises for the same muscle) are an extreme metabolic stimulus but very fatiguing.

Rest-Pause: Perform a set to near-failure, rest 10–15 seconds, perform another mini-set to failure, repeat 2–3 times. Allows near-maximum motor unit recruitment across multiple mini-sets. Core technique of Dorian Yates' HIT approach. Use sparingly — very demanding.

Mechanical Drop Sets: Rather than reducing weight, switch to a biomechanically easier variation. E.g. close-grip pull-up → neutral-grip pull-up → assisted pull-up. Continues work with similar load when the harder variation is exhausted.

Tempo Manipulation: Slowing the eccentric phase to 3–5 seconds increases time under tension and muscle damage in the stretch position. E.g. 3-0-1-0 tempo. Useful for beginners developing mind-muscle connection and for isolation exercises to increase stimulus without adding load.

Blood Flow Restriction (BFR): Apply a cuff to the proximal limb at ~50–80% arterial occlusion pressure. Train with very light loads (20–30% 1RM) for high reps (15–30). Metabolite accumulation and cell swelling produce a strong hypertrophic stimulus at very light loads. Used in rehab, post-surgery, or as additional volume without joint stress.

Bulk & Cut Phases
Periodised nutrition and training for long-term bodybuilding progress
Periodisation
▼

Bulking (Caloric Surplus): A mild caloric surplus (250–500 kcal/day) allows anabolic signalling pathways to operate at maximum while minimising fat gain. "Dirty bulking" (very large surplus) accelerates fat gain disproportionately to muscle gain and creates more extreme cuts. During a bulk: higher volume, progressive overload is easiest to achieve.

Cutting (Caloric Deficit): Goal is fat loss while preserving maximum muscle. Key strategies: maintain training intensity (keep lifting heavy — muscle is preserved when the body senses it's still needed), increase protein intake (1.8–2.4g/kg body weight), prioritise sleep, manage cardio carefully (too much LISS is catabolic; HIIT preserves muscle better). During a cut: reduce volume slightly, maintain intensity, do not chase new PRs.

Body Recomposition: Simultaneous muscle gain and fat loss is possible in: beginners (significant adaptation headroom), returning trainees after a break (muscle memory), and those with excess body fat who are relative novices. Requires precise caloric maintenance with high protein and consistent progressive training. Less effective the more advanced the trainee.

HIT — High Intensity Training (Mentzer / Yates)
One set to true failure — polarising, effective for some, not optimal for all
Low VolumeHigh Intensity
▼

Mike Mentzer and Dorian Yates (6× Mr. Olympia) popularised HIT: one set taken to complete muscular failure, using rest-pause and forced reps, is sufficient to stimulate maximum hypertrophy — additional sets are counterproductive to recovery. Training frequency is very low (each body part once every 5–7 days).

The argument for HIT: If you have stimulated maximum growth with one set to failure, every additional set burns resources needed for recovery without adding stimulus. Dorian Yates built one of the most impressive physiques in bodybuilding history using this approach with heavy compound movements taken to true failure.

The argument against: Modern volume research supports 10–20+ hard sets per muscle per week for maximum hypertrophy. One set reaches the Minimum Effective Stimulus but not the Maximum Adaptive Volume. HIT works — particularly for intermediate/advanced trainees with excellent mind-muscle connection — but leaves gains on the table for those who can recover from more volume.

80/20 Training — Polarised Model
The training distribution used by elite endurance athletes worldwide
Elite Model
▼
Zone 1–2

Zone 3
Minimised — "no man's land"

Research by Dr. Stephen Seiler analysing elite endurance athletes (runners, cyclists, rowers, cross-country skiers) consistently found ~80% of training at low intensity (below lactate threshold 1) and ~20% at high intensity (above lactate threshold 2). Zone 3 — moderate intensity — is largely avoided.

Why avoid zone 3? Moderate-intensity training is physiologically costly (significant fatigue accumulation) but produces less adaptation per unit of fatigue than either low or high intensity. It's "too hard to recover from quickly, not hard enough to be maximally stimulating." Athletes who train primarily at moderate intensity often plateau.

Practical application: The majority of running/cycling/rowing should feel fully conversational. The hard 20% should be genuinely hard — intervals, tempo runs, time trials. The most common recreational athlete mistake is training everything at moderate effort — harder than easy, not hard enough to qualify as high intensity.

Marathon / Long Distance Running
Base building, long runs, speed work, and race-day peaking
Running
▼

Key principles: Maximum 10% weekly mileage increase (injury prevention). Long run = 20–25% of weekly mileage. Most running at easy pace (60–70% max HR). Introduce speed work only after 4–6 weeks of base building. Include minimum one rest day. Strength training (single-leg work, hip stability, calf strength) reduces injury risk by 50%+ according to meta-analyses.

Training run types: Easy runs (daily recovery), long run (weekly, building to 18–22 miles pre-marathon), tempo runs (comfortably hard, around lactate threshold — roughly 60-minute race pace), intervals (5K–10K race pace, 400m–1200m repeats), progression runs (start easy, finish at marathon pace or faster).

Taper: Final 2–3 weeks before race: reduce mileage 20–30% per week. Maintain intensity, reduce volume. Race week: mostly easy running. Allows recovery and glycogen supercompensation. Many first-time marathoners make the mistake of doing too much in the final weeks out of anxiety — trust the taper.

Triathlon Training
Managing three disciplines, brick workouts, and multi-sport periodisation
Multi-Sport
▼

Structure: Train all three disciplines across the week. Weekly hours by distance: Sprint (6–8h), Olympic (8–12h), Half-Ironman (12–16h), Ironman (16–22h). Managing fatigue across disciplines is the art of triathlon coaching — overload in one affects the others. Typically swim 3–4x, bike 3–5x, run 4–5x for Ironman preparation.

Brick workouts: Two disciplines back-to-back. Bike + run bricks are most important — the "jelly legs" experienced running off the bike requires specific neuromuscular adaptation. Start with short run bricks (10–15 min) immediately after bike sessions, build to race-simulation long bricks. Swim-to-bike transitions are less physiologically demanding.

Injury management: Running creates the most musculoskeletal stress. Swimming is the most joint-friendly. Eccentric calf and hamstring work, hip stability, and progressive run mileage are crucial. Many triathletes under-run in training relative to swim/bike due to injury risk — running fitness is hardest to build and easiest to lose.

VO₂ max is the maximum rate of oxygen consumption during maximal exercise. It is the gold standard measure of cardiovascular fitness. To train VO₂ max, you must spend time at or near VO₂ max intensity — roughly the pace you could sustain for 6–8 minutes all-out.

Classic protocols: Norwegian 4×4 (4 sets of 4 minutes at 90–95% max HR, 3 minutes active recovery between). 5×3 minutes at true all-out effort with 3 minutes rest. 8×2 minutes. The Norwegian interval model (high volume, high-quality interval training) has produced the most world-class endurance athletes of the past two decades — Jakob Ingebrigtsen, Kristian Blummenfelt, Gustav Iden.

CrossFit
Constantly varied, high-intensity functional movement across all physical domains
General Fitness
▼

CrossFit's definition of fitness: maximising work capacity across broad time and modal domains. It aims to develop all 10 physical skills: cardiovascular endurance, stamina, strength, flexibility, power, speed, coordination, agility, balance, and accuracy. WODs (Workout of the Day) combine Olympic weightlifting, gymnastics (pull-ups, handstand push-ups, muscle-ups), and metabolic conditioning in ever-changing combinations.

Named workouts (Girls/Heroes): Fran (21-15-9 thrusters + pull-ups), Cindy (AMRAP 20 min: 5 pull-ups, 10 push-ups, 15 squats), Murph (1 mile run, 100 pull-ups, 200 push-ups, 300 squats, 1 mile run, weighted vest). Benchmark WODs allow progress tracking over time.

Strengths: Develops broad general fitness. Strong community and competitive element increases adherence. Exposure to Olympic lifts and gymnastics movements most gym-goers never attempt. High intensity produces significant cardiovascular and strength adaptations.

Limitations: Random programming without structured progressive overload limits strength and hypertrophy compared to dedicated training. High injury risk when Olympic lifts are performed under fatigue without adequate technique. Programming quality varies enormously between boxes. Not optimal for any single quality — a specialist will outperform a CrossFitter in their domain.

Kettlebell Training
Ballistic and grind movements for total body strength and conditioning
Versatile
▼

The swing: The fundamental kettlebell movement. A hip-hinge pattern loaded with a ballistic (explosive) eccentric. Develops posterior chain power, grip strength, and aerobic/anaerobic conditioning simultaneously. Russian swing (eye level) vs. American swing (overhead) — Russian is safer for high-rep conditioning; American requires more shoulder mobility.

Grind movements: Turkish Get-Up (total body integration, shoulder stability, mobility — arguably the best single exercise for overall movement quality), Goblet Squat (squat teaching tool, anterior-loaded hip mobility), Kettlebell Press, Bent-Over Row, Single-Leg Deadlift.

Ballistic movements: Swing, Clean, Snatch, Jerk. The kettlebell snatch (floor to overhead in one explosion) is one of the most demanding conditioning movements. The Secret Service Snatch Test: 10-minute snatch test with a 24 kg bell — a benchmark of extraordinary grip and conditioning.

Pavel Tsatsouline (StrongFirst): "Practice, not workout" — every set should be crisp and deliberate, far from failure. Goal is strength and skill development, not metabolic destruction. "Grease the groove" — frequent practice at moderate intensity to develop motor patterns without fatigue.

Calisthenics / Gymnastics Strength Training
Mastering bodyweight — from push-ups to planche and front lever
Bodyweight
▼

Calisthenics uses bodyweight as resistance, progressed through increasingly difficult skill variations rather than external load. Push skill hierarchy: push-up → archer push-up → pseudo planche push-up → planche. Pull hierarchy: row → pull-up → L-sit pull-up → muscle-up → front lever. Squat: squat → pistol squat → shrimp squat.

Rings: Gymnastic rings add instability that dramatically increases recruitment compared to bar work. Ring dips, ring push-ups, ring muscle-ups, ring support holds. The false grip (wrist on top of ring for muscle-up transition) is one of the most important skills to develop for ring training.

Isometric skills: Planche (horizontal body held parallel to ground on hands), front lever, back lever, human flag, L-sit. These require extreme strength-to-weight ratios and develop qualities unachievable with weighted training alone. Progressions must be programmed methodically over months.

Other Training Methods

Metabolic Resistance Training (MRT)
Maximum caloric expenditure and conditioning — compound circuits with minimal rest
▼

MRT combines resistance training movements performed in circuits with minimal rest — creating both a muscular and cardiovascular stimulus simultaneously. Ideal for general fitness, fat loss, and time-constrained clients. Example: 5 exercises, 40s on / 20s off × 4 rounds — squat, push-up, kettlebell swing, dumbbell row, burpee.

EPOC (Excess Post-Exercise Oxygen Consumption): High-intensity training elevates metabolic rate for 24–48 hours post-exercise. The "afterburn" is real but often overstated in marketing — typically adds 50–150 kcal extra. The primary value of MRT is during-session caloric expenditure and cardiovascular adaptation, not afterburn magnitude alone.

Best for: General fitness clients with limited time (<45 minutes), those who enjoy variety and group training formats, fat loss phases where maintaining training efficiency is priority, and clients who don't respond well to traditional gym formats.

Olympic Weightlifting
The snatch and clean & jerk — pure expression of total body athletic power
▼

Olympic weightlifting (OWL) as a sport focuses exclusively on the snatch (bar from floor to overhead in one movement) and the clean & jerk (floor to shoulder, then overhead in a second movement). Athletes are classified by bodyweight. These are the most technically demanding movements in sport — requiring years of dedicated coaching.

Training structure: Heavy emphasis on competition movements and variations (power snatch, hang clean, snatch from blocks). Significant back squat and front squat work (required to receive the bar in the bottom position). Accessory work: snatch grip deadlift, overhead squat, good morning, pulling variations.

As an S&C tool: Even partial OWL movements (power clean, hang clean, push press) are valuable for athletic populations — hip extension power and triple extension transfer directly to sprint mechanics and jumping. Do not attempt to teach beginners the full snatch without dedicated coaching time — technical errors under load are the primary injury mechanism.

Strongman Training
Odd-object lifting, loaded carries, and functional real-world strength
▼

Competition events: Atlas stones (loading round stones onto progressively higher platforms), log press, farmer's walk, tyre flip, yoke carry, axle bar deadlift, car deadlift. Each event demands a different physical quality — the sport requires the most well-rounded strength of any strength sport.

Training benefits beyond competition: Loaded carries (farmer's walk, suitcase carry, yoke, sandbag) are arguably the most functional strength exercises in existence — they develop grip strength, core stability, upper back development, and real-world load-bearing capacity simultaneously. Sandbag and keg work develops awkward-object strength that transfers directly to occupational and tactical settings.

For general population clients: Farmer's walks, sandbag carries, tyre flips, and sled dragging add variety, develop functional strength patterns, and are typically highly engaging for clients who find traditional barbell work monotonous. Tyre flips are particularly effective for group training energy and team cohesion.

Yoga & Pilates
Mobility, stability, mind-muscle connection, and active recovery modalities
▼

Yoga: Develops mobility, balance, proprioception, and breath control. Ashtanga (dynamic, physically demanding — sun salutations and standing postures), Hatha (slower, held postures), Yin (long holds targeting deep connective tissue — fascia, ligaments). Valuable for athletes as active recovery and mobility maintenance. Breath control (pranayama) has documented benefits for stress regulation and HRV improvement.

Pilates: Focuses on deep stabiliser activation (transversus abdominis, multifidus, pelvic floor), controlled movement through full range, and postural alignment. Reformer Pilates adds spring-loaded resistance. Used extensively in athletic rehab, dance training, and postpartum recovery. Highly relevant for clients with lower back pain or poor core activation patterns.

For coaches: Yoga and Pilates are underutilised recovery and mobility tools for hard-training athletes. A weekly yoga session significantly reduces injury risk, improves movement quality, and provides a mental deload from high-intensity training. Recommend to clients who resist traditional stretching and mobility work — the structured class format provides accountability.

Rehabilitation & Return-to-Sport
Progressive loading through injury and back to full performance
▼

Phases of rehab: Phase 1 — Acute (pain management, POLICE: Protect, Optimal Loading, Ice, Compression, Elevation). Phase 2 — Subacute (restore ROM, light resistance, neuromuscular re-education). Phase 3 — Strength rebuilding (progressive loading, address compensations). Phase 4 — Power and sport-specific (plyometrics, agility, sport movements). Phase 5 — Return to full training and competition.

Criteria-based return to sport: Rather than time-based return ("6 weeks post-surgery"), use performance criteria: <10% side-to-side strength deficit, ≥90% limb symmetry index in functional tests (single-leg hop tests, single-leg squat assessment), no pain under load, psychological readiness. Time is a guide, not a gate.

Common injuries for coaches: ACL rehab (9–12 months minimum; quad-dominant compensation is the primary re-injury risk — eccentric quad and hip control work is essential), hamstring strains (Nordic curls as primary prevention and rehab), shoulder impingement (scapular stability, rotator cuff strengthening, posterior capsule stretching), lower back pain (usually movement quality and loading tolerance, not structural — address with movement re-education and progressive loading rather than avoidance).

Tactical Fitness — Military & Emergency Services
Occupational performance training for physically demanding professions
▼

Tactical athletes (military, police, fire, paramedics) require sustained performance under fatigue, load carriage, and the ability to perform physical tasks after prolonged inactivity. Different from sport athletes — recovery between "events" may be hours of inactivity followed by explosive demands under stress.

Key physical qualities: Load carriage (Bergen/ruck marching — metabolic and musculoskeletal), upper and lower body strength (casualty evacuation, equipment handling), aerobic base for sustained operations, and injury resilience for occupational longevity. Emphasis on work capacity under load over aesthetic or single-quality sports performance.

Programming: Mountain Tactical Institute (MTI) and GORUCK methodologies specifically address tactical athlete needs. Emphasis on ruck-specific conditioning, loaded carry capacity, and balanced fitness. Job-specific fitness tests (infantry fitness tests, fire service physical competency assessments) are programming targets.

Concurrent Training
Combining strength and endurance in the same programme — managing the interference effect
▼

The interference effect: Training both strength and endurance simultaneously can blunt adaptations to both — the AMPK pathway activated by endurance training can inhibit the mTOR pathway required for muscle growth. This is the "interference effect." However, it is often overstated — particularly at moderate volumes, concurrent training is highly effective for most people who are not competing at the elite level in either quality.

Minimising interference: Separate strength and endurance sessions by as much time as possible (ideally 6+ hours or different days). Prioritise the quality most important to the client's goal in the first session of the day. Avoid heavy leg strength work immediately before or after high-intensity running — the fatigue combination significantly impairs both quality of sessions. HIIT creates more interference than LISS.

Practical example: Morning: strength training. Afternoon/evening: easy aerobic work (zone 2). This order and timing minimises interference while developing both qualities. Most general fitness clients benefit from concurrent training — its optimal management matters most for competitive athletes.

HYROX is a standardised fitness race held worldwide — the same format at every event globally. Competitors run 1km then complete a functional exercise station, alternating for 8 rounds. The 8 stations are always: SkiErg (1000m), Sled Push (50m, weighted), Sled Pull (50m, weighted), Burpee Broad Jumps (80m), Rowing (1000m), Farmers Carry (200m), Sandbag Lunges (100m), Wall Balls (100 reps, 6kg women / 9kg men). Physical demands: Aerobic capacity (for the 8×1km runs — VO₂ max matters enormously), muscular endurance (particularly legs, posterior chain, shoulders), grip strength (farmers carry, sled), and lactate threshold (sustaining high output through accumulated fatigue). The unique challenge is maintaining running pace after each functional station — your legs are pre-fatigued going into every run leg.

Training approach — 12-week HYROX prep block:

Weeks 1–4 (Base): Build aerobic base (3–4 zone 2 runs/week), introduce station-specific movements with moderate load and volume. Focus on technique — sled mechanics, ski erg efficiency, wall ball rhythm. Strength sessions 2× per week focusing on posterior chain (deadlift, RDL, hip thrust) and shoulders (overhead press, lateral raise).

Weeks 5–8 (Build): Introduce race simulation — run 1km then immediately perform a station exercise at race weight. Brick sessions build the sport-specific fatigue pattern. Increase running volume. Add interval running (400m repeats at race pace + 30 seconds). Practice transitions between running and exercises.

Weeks 9–12 (Race Specific): Full simulation sessions (4–6 station HYROX simulations with running). Peak then taper. Race pace running. Reduce volume by 30% in final 2 weeks. Race week: short activation sessions only — 20-minute easy run, light station work to maintain neuromuscular patterns without fatigue.

Weekly structure (in-training): Mon — long run (8–12km zone 2). Tue — strength (posterior chain + shoulders). Wed — HYROX simulation session (run + stations). Thu — recovery/mobility. Fri — interval running + station repeats. Sat — long simulation or race. Sun — rest.

Key performance tips: Pace the sled push conservatively — it destroys more competitors than any other station. Wall balls: find a rhythm and don't break until the end. Ski erg: damper setting 4–5, strong pull-through with hips. Farmers carry: walk fast, don't jog. The final 1km run after all 8 stations is a pure test of character — train for it specifically.

REC

Recovery — The Most Underrated Training Variable

Why Recovery Is Training

Adaptation does not occur during training — it occurs during recovery. Training is a controlled stressor that creates a demand for adaptation. Sleep, nutrition, and active recovery are when that adaptation is actually built. A coach who only programmes training and ignores recovery is leaving significant results on the table. Many "plateau" situations are actually under-recovery situations in disguise.

Sleep — The Foundation of All Recovery
The single most important recovery tool available — free and often neglected
▼

Sleep is the most potent recovery, anabolic, and cognitive restoration tool that exists. The majority of growth hormone is secreted during slow-wave sleep (stages 3–4). Testosterone production is strongly correlated with sleep duration — one week of sleeping 5 hours/night reduces testosterone by 10–15% in young men. Muscle protein synthesis occurs predominantly during sleep. Memory consolidation of motor patterns learned during training happens during REM sleep.

Target: 7–9 hours per night for the general population. Athletes in heavy training blocks benefit from 8–10 hours plus daytime naps where possible. Elite athlete programmes frequently include mandatory nap protocols.

Sleep quality optimisation: Consistent sleep and wake times (even weekends — circadian rhythm is critical). Room temperature 16–19°C (cooler is better for sleep quality). Complete darkness — blackout curtains or sleep mask. No screens 60 minutes before bed (blue light suppresses melatonin). No caffeine after 2pm. Alcohol severely disrupts sleep architecture — even moderate amounts reduce deep sleep by 20–30%.

Supplements for sleep quality: Magnesium glycinate (300–400mg before bed) — significantly improves deep sleep quality. Melatonin (0.3–1mg) — for shifting sleep timing or jet lag, not as a nightly crutch. Ashwagandha (300–600mg KSM-66) — reduces cortisol and improves sleep quality over 4–8 weeks.

Active Recovery
Low-intensity movement that accelerates recovery without adding fatigue
▼

Active recovery — light movement at 40–60% max heart rate — accelerates clearance of metabolic waste products (lactate, hydrogen ions), increases blood flow to recovering muscles, and maintains neuromuscular activation without adding meaningful fatigue. Complete rest is often inferior to active recovery for speeding next-session readiness.

Active recovery options: 20–30 minute walk (most accessible — dramatically underestimated for recovery), light cycling at zone 1–2, swimming (particularly effective — low impact, full body blood flow, hydrostatic pressure reduces inflammation), yoga or light stretching, foam rolling and mobility work, light recreational sport.

Active recovery structure: Duration 20–40 minutes. Heart rate below 130 bpm (should feel genuinely easy — conversational pace throughout). Never push through fatigue during active recovery — if it feels hard, slow down. The goal is blood flow and parasympathetic nervous system activation, not fitness stimulus.

HRV (Heart Rate Variability) monitoring: HRV is the gold standard readiness metric. High HRV = well-recovered parasympathetic state (ready for hard training). Low HRV = sympathetically loaded (needs more recovery or reduced intensity). Apps: HRV4Training, Elite HRV, WHOOP, Garmin HRV Status. Measure first thing in the morning, same time daily before getting up. Track trends over weeks — a consistently declining HRV signals accumulated fatigue requiring a deload.

Soft Tissue Work — Foam Rolling, Massage & Myofascial Release
Restoring tissue quality, reducing DOMS, and improving movement patterns
▼

Foam rolling and massage work on the myofascial system — the connective tissue network surrounding and permeating muscle tissue. The primary evidence-backed benefits: reduced perception of muscle soreness (DOMS), improved range of motion acutely, increased parasympathetic nervous system activity (relaxation response), and psychological recovery. The evidence for foam rolling directly accelerating muscle protein synthesis or preventing injury is weaker than often claimed — but the ROM and soreness benefits are consistent.

Foam rolling protocol: Spend 30–60 seconds on each muscle group. Move slowly (1 inch per second) rather than fast back-and-forth. When you find a tender spot, hold pressure for 20–30 seconds and breathe slowly into it. Key areas for most athletes: thoracic spine, quads, ITB/TFL, piriformis, calves, lats. Always roll with the muscle relaxed — do not roll on injured tissue or directly over joints.

Sports massage: Professional sports massage provides deeper tissue work, trigger point release, and fascial manipulation not achievable with foam rolling. Recommended frequency: once every 1–2 weeks during heavy training blocks, once a month for general maintenance. Deep tissue work can temporarily increase muscle soreness for 24–48 hours before providing relief — schedule at least 48 hours before a high-demand training session.

Percussive therapy (Theragun/Hypervolt): High-frequency vibration into muscle tissue reduces DOMS and improves blood flow. Best used post-training for 60–90 seconds per muscle group. Not a replacement for traditional massage but an effective and convenient adjunct.

Thermal Recovery — Ice, Heat & Contrast Therapy
Managing inflammation, blood flow, and the recovery response
▼

Cold water immersion (CWI / ice bath): 10–15 minutes at 10–15°C post-training. Reduces acute inflammation, decreases DOMS perception, blunts neuromuscular fatigue. Most effective for reducing soreness when the priority is match performance 24–48 hours later. Caveat: CWI blunts the inflammatory response that drives some long-term adaptations — avoid ice baths immediately after strength training sessions where hypertrophy is the goal (the inflammation is part of the adaptation signal). Use strategically — primarily for sport performance recovery, not post-hypertrophy training.

Heat therapy (sauna, hot bath): Increases blood flow, reduces muscle tension, promotes parasympathetic response, and elevates heat shock proteins (which aid cellular repair). Post-session sauna (15–20 minutes at 80–100°C) has evidence for cardiovascular adaptation and growth hormone release. Hot bath with Epsom salts (magnesium sulphate) — transdermal magnesium absorption is debated but the heat benefit alone is valuable for muscle relaxation.

Contrast therapy: Alternating hot and cold (3 min hot / 1 min cold × 3–4 cycles). Creates a "pumping" effect in vasculature — alternating vasodilation (heat) and vasoconstriction (cold) flushes metabolic waste from tissue. Widely used by professional sports teams. Practical at home: alternate hot and cold shower cycles. More evidence for reducing soreness and fatigue than either modality alone.

Nutrition for Recovery
Fuelling the recovery process — what to eat and when
▼

Post-workout nutrition window: Within 1–2 hours post-training, consume 30–40g protein (to maximally stimulate MPS) + 0.5–1g/kg body weight carbohydrates (to begin glycogen replenishment). This window matters more for those training twice daily or within 8 hours — for once-daily training, total daily intake is the priority, but getting nutrients in post-workout is still excellent practice.

Anti-inflammatory nutrition: Chronic inflammation impairs recovery. Foods with strong anti-inflammatory properties: oily fish (omega-3 EPA/DHA), berries (polyphenols, anthocyanins), tart cherry juice (anthocyanins — strong evidence for reducing DOMS and accelerating recovery), turmeric with black pepper (curcumin + piperine), ginger, dark leafy greens. Avoid excessive processed foods, seed oils, and alcohol — all pro-inflammatory.

Tart cherry juice: One of the best-evidenced single food interventions for recovery. 30ml concentrated tart cherry juice twice daily for 4–5 days around an intense event significantly reduces DOMS and accelerates strength recovery. Used by distance runners, rugby players, and MMA athletes. Practical, affordable, and effective.

Hydration: Even mild dehydration (1–2% body weight) significantly impairs recovery, cognitive function, and next-session performance. Aim to begin exercise well-hydrated and replace losses post-session — approximately 1.5L per kg of body weight lost during exercise (weigh before and after long sessions). Electrolytes matter for extended sessions — sodium (in sports drinks or electrolyte tablets) is the critical electrolyte for fluid retention and muscle function.

Psychological Recovery & Stress Management
The nervous system recovers last — managing total life stress for training readiness
▼

The nervous system cannot differentiate between training stress and life stress — both draw from the same recovery pool. A client under extreme work, relationship, or financial stress will not recover from the same training load they handle easily in calm periods. This is one of the most practically important things a coach can understand: prescribe training based on total life stress, not just training history.

Parasympathetic activation practices: Diaphragmatic (belly) breathing — 4 counts in, hold 4, out 6. Box breathing (4-4-4-4). Meditation (even 10 minutes daily improves HRV and recovery metrics over time). Cold water exposure (face submerged in cold water activates the dive reflex — powerful parasympathetic activation). Walking in nature — well-evidenced for cortisol reduction and mental recovery.

Overtraining syndrome (OTS): A persistent state of maladaptation from excessive training stress relative to recovery. Symptoms: declining performance despite continued training, persistent fatigue, mood disturbance (increased depression, anxiety, irritability), sleep disruption, increased illness frequency, hormonal disruption (low testosterone, high cortisol). Treatment: significant reduction or complete cessation of training for weeks to months, addressing sleep and nutrition, managing psychological stressors. Prevention is far superior to treatment — deloads and periodic testing catch overreaching before it becomes OTS.

Periodisation

What is Periodisation?

Periodisation is the systematic planning of athletic training — varying volume, intensity, and specificity over time to maximise performance at the right moment while minimising overtraining and injury. Every coach should understand the major models and know when to apply each one.

Linear Periodisation
Progressive weekly increase in intensity, decrease in volume — the classic model
▼

The simplest model. Each week or block increases intensity (% 1RM) while decreasing volume (total sets × reps). Classic 12-week powerlifting prep: Weeks 1–4 (Hypertrophy): 4×10 at 65–70%. Weeks 5–8 (Strength): 4×5 at 75–80%. Weeks 9–11 (Power/Peaking): 3×3 at 85–90%. Week 12: Taper and test.

Best for: Beginners and early intermediates — predictability and simplicity make it highly effective when someone still responds to basic progressive overload. Athletes peaking for a specific competition date. Limitation: Accommodates to a single stimulus. Advanced trainees plateau quickly when only one quality is trained at a time.

Undulating Periodisation (DUP / WUP)
Varying rep ranges within the week for concurrent adaptation
▼

Daily Undulating Periodisation (DUP): Rep ranges change every session. Monday: strength (3×5 at 85%). Wednesday: power (5×3 at 70% for speed). Friday: hypertrophy (4×10 at 67%). The same muscle/movement is trained 3x/week with different rep/intensity profiles. Avoids accommodation while maintaining high frequency. Research shows DUP produces superior strength and hypertrophy gains compared to linear periodisation in intermediate trainees over 8–12 week periods.

Weekly Undulating Periodisation (WUP): Rep ranges change week to week — wave loading. Week 1: 5s, Week 2: 3s, Week 3: back to 5s but heavier. Provides variety over slightly longer blocks while being simpler to programme than DUP.

Best for: Intermediate–advanced trainees who have plateaued on linear periodisation. Standard tool in evidence-based strength and hypertrophy programming.

Block Periodisation
Sequential focused blocks — accumulation, transmutation, realisation
▼

Block periodisation (Vladimir Issurin) organises training into concentrated 3–6 week blocks (mesocycles), each with a dominant focus: Accumulation (high volume, general fitness, hypertrophy) → Transmutation (convert volume to intensity, sport-specific strength, power) → Realisation (peak and compete, taper, maximum expression of built qualities).

Residual training effects: Different qualities retain adaptations for different durations after training stops: aerobic endurance (30± days), max strength (30± days), anaerobic glycolytic capacity (18± days), speed-strength (5± days). Block periodisation sequences blocks to exploit these — training speed-strength last before competition because it fades fastest and must be expressed immediately.

Best for: Advanced athletes with a specific competition season. Widely used in team sports (pre-season = accumulation, in-season = transmutation + realisation), powerlifting, and Olympic sports.

Deload Weeks
Planned reductions in training stress to allow supercompensation and prevent overtraining
▼

A deload is a planned reduction in training volume and/or intensity (typically 40–50%) for 1 week after 3–6 weeks of progressive loading. Purpose: dissipate accumulated fatigue, allow full recovery and supercompensation, reduce overuse injury risk, and maintain psychological freshness. Adaptations often become fully expressed after a deload — athletes frequently hit PRs in the week following a proper deload.

Types: Volume deload (reduce sets 40–50%, keep intensity high). Intensity deload (reduce load 10–20%, keep volume). Complete rest (rarely necessary — more appropriate post-competition). Active recovery (light movement, mobility, sport activities — most practical for maintaining movement patterns).

Deload indicators: HRV (heart rate variability) consistently dropping despite adequate sleep and nutrition. Sleep quality deterioration. Persistent soreness beyond normal. Declining performance. Joint pain. Motivation loss. Don't wait for these signs — programme deloads proactively every 4–8 weeks based on training intensity.

Client Assessment Checklist

1. Goal clarification: What does the client actually want? Be specific. "I want to lose 10kg" vs. "I want to feel fit" require different approaches. Distinguish between outcome goals (lose 10kg) and process goals (train 4x/week, eat 2g protein/kg).

2. Training history: How long have they been training? What have they done? What worked, what didn't? What do they enjoy? Adherence is the most important variable — the best programme is the one they'll do.

3. Movement screen: Basic movement quality — can they hinge, squat, push, pull, and carry with adequate form? Identify limitations before loading. Use FMS or your own systematic movement assessment. Address limitations before adding intensity.

4. Schedule and constraints: Days realistically available? Time per session? Equipment access? Injuries or medical conditions? Travel frequency? Gym preference? Home training option?

5. Recovery factors: Sleep quality and quantity, life stress levels, nutrition habits (sufficient protein?), alcohol intake. All affect training capacity and the rate of adaptation. A client sleeping 5 hours/night needs a different volume prescription than one sleeping 8 hours.

Common Coaching Errors

Too much too soon: Beginners do not need 6-day splits, drop sets, or advanced techniques. A simple 3-day full body with the Big Four movements and progressive overload is optimal for the first 6–12 months.

Ignoring recovery: Adding more sessions when a client plateaus is usually the wrong answer. More often the issue is insufficient sleep, nutrition, stress, or inadequate deloading.

Exercise over movement pattern: Build programmes around movement patterns (push, pull, hinge, squat, carry) then fill in exercises — not the reverse. This ensures balance and allows exercise substitution without redesigning the programme.

Neglecting the posterior chain: Most clients over-develop the anterior chain (quad, chest, anterior delt) and under-develop the posterior (hamstring, glute, upper back, rear delt). Pull:push ratio should be at least 1:1, ideally 2:1.

Programming the coach's favourite exercises: Programme for the client's body, goals, and movement quality — not the exercises you enjoy or find impressive. A goblet squat is a better starting point for most clients than a back squat.

Progressive Overload — All Methods

Progressive overload doesn't mean "add weight every week." There are multiple ways to increase training stimulus — understanding them allows you to programme intelligently when weight progression stalls:

Load: Increase weight lifted. Most obvious — applicable early in training; becomes harder to achieve consistently as the trainee advances.

Volume: Add sets or reps at the same load. 3×8 → 3×10 → 4×10 → 4×12 is a valid and underutilised progression block for intermediate trainees.

Density: Same work in less time (reduce rest periods). Cutting rest from 90s to 75s while keeping load and reps constant is meaningful overload. Increases metabolic stress and time efficiency.

Range of motion: Progress to a deeper squat, fuller ROM Romanian deadlift, or deficit push-up. Increases mechanical challenge without adding weight. Particularly important for developing hypertrophy in the lengthened position.

Exercise difficulty: Progress from goblet squat → barbell back squat, or dumbbell row → barbell row — a more demanding variation of the same pattern requiring greater stability and neuromuscular coordination.

Frequency: Train the same muscle or pattern more times per week. Moving from 1x/week to 2x/week training a muscle group is a significant volume increase spread across more frequent protein synthesis windows.

Tempo: Introduce a 3-second eccentric where the client previously used no controlled descent. Dramatically increases time under tension without changing the load — particularly useful when load cannot increase (injury, deload, or plateau).

Warm-Up Protocol — The 4-Phase Model

Phase 1 — Raise (3–5 min): Elevate heart rate, increase muscle temperature and synovial fluid viscosity. Light cardio: bike, row, skip. Not enough to fatigue — simply prepare the system for what follows.

Phase 2 — Mobilise (5–8 min): Active mobility work targeting the specific joints and ranges required in today's session. Hip CARs and 90/90 before squatting. Thoracic rotation before pressing and golf work. Ankle dorsiflexion before deep knee bend patterns. Dynamic rather than static — static stretching pre-session reduces force production transiently.

Phase 3 — Activate (3–5 min): Neuromuscular activation of key muscles that tend to underfire. Banded clamshells and glute bridges before lower body work. Band pull-aparts and face pulls before pressing. Dead bugs and Pallof press for core activation. These prime the movement patterns before loading.

Phase 4 — Potentiate (2–3 min): Progressive build-up sets of the first main exercise. 50% × 5, 70% × 3, 85% × 1, then into working sets. The potentiation phase ensures the neuromuscular system is fully prepared for maximal or near-maximal effort. Never skip warm-up sets on compound movements regardless of time pressure.
